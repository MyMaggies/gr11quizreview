HOW FAST FASHION IS A TOXIC TREND

1. We've long understood that advertising, magazines and celebrity culture damage teens' sense of financial responsibility by enforcing a 'buy more' mentality. Lesser known is the impact of fast fashion on environmental awareness and ethical consumption. With the explosive growth of online shopping platforms, most parents spend considerable time managing delivery notifications and addressing impulse purchases, making sure their kids think before they buy and understand the consequences of disposable fashion. For at least a decade, educators like me have argued that fast fashion's biggest threat was its ability to filch money from teenagers with the lure of cheap, trendy clothing that falls apart after minimal wear.

2. Not anymore. Fast fashion has also become a toxic cycle.

3. Earlier this year, environmental scientists found robust cross-cultural evidence linking fast fashion consumption to resource depletion, waste accumulation, exploitative labor practices, and significant carbon emissions worldwide. Note: that doesn't mean buying affordable clothing causes all these problems, but that there's a strong association between them.

4. Retail platforms like SHEIN, Fashion Nova and Temu deliver the tools that allow teens to constantly refresh their wardrobes and participate in micro-trends that last mere weeks. The most vulnerable consumers, researchers say, are the ones who spend most of their time browsing, purchasing multiple hauls weekly, and posting outfit content online. One study found that teenage shoppers who did this on these platforms were more likely to link their self-worth to their appearance and trend participation. Interestingly, whilst girls report more fashion shopping, studies have shown that members of both genders can be equally affected by compulsive buying behaviors.

5. And thanks to an array of mobile apps and one-click purchasing, fast fashion retailers now have the power to reach consumers in ways that are practically on par with addictive social media platforms. If the Internet has been called a great marketplace, perhaps what fast fashion companies have done is let anyone enter the constant style refresh cycle. Teens can browse thousands of items, receive personalized recommendations and track shipping in real-time with the swipe of a finger, curating their own image to become trendier, more stylish and more 'relevant'.

6. All this provides an illusion of empowerment: if I spend more time shopping and really build my wardrobe, I can express myself. 'I don't get to choose my body type or natural features,' one young woman told me. 'If I could, my proportions would be different. But I can choose which outfit makes me look better.'

7. But invariably, the line between self-expression and consumption becomes blurred. 'I think it affects teens subconsciously, just seeing how quickly trends change and feeling like their clothes are never good enough,' one 16-year-old told me.

8. What teens purchase through fast fashion is overshadowed by what they ignore. Pre-Internet, you had to travel to physical stores to find affordable clothing ‚Äì a process that required effort and limited spontaneous purchases. Now the barriers are as minimal as they are enticing. Teens can scroll endlessly through new arrivals whilst garment workers in distant factories labor in dangerous conditions. Quality concerns vanish in favor of quantity. Environmental impact gets dismissed. The stigmatisation of wearing the same outfit twice becomes normalized, reinforcing the endless buying cycle.

9. The meteoric rise of ultra-fast fashion online has created an entire culture of 'haul' videos and disposable trends. Millions of followers embrace frequent purchasing for entertainment and validation, increasingly treating clothing as single-use rather than long-term investment. This year, an analysis of 50 major fashion influencer accounts revealed content that was indistinguishable, at times, from promotion of wasteful consumption or environmental negligence. Both contained strong messaging encouraging weekly shopping sprees and linking personal value to constant wardrobe updates, and promoted the idea that affordable prices justify unlimited purchasing. Writing in Vogue, 24-year-old sustainability advocate Emma Rodriguez recounted how an obsession with fast fashion enabled her to ignore her growing debt and overflowing closet of unworn items. 'I had found my style,' she wrote. 'I was not thinking about consequences.'

10. Many teens are environmentally aware about climate change: they can identify problems like pollution with a critical eye. Less clear is how environmental literacy translates to understanding their own consumption patterns. The older adolescents I work with often dismiss conversations about fashion's ecological impact with a 'whatever' or 'I know that already'. That doesn't mean they're not listening, or feeling conflicted about their purchasing habits. (Besides, this population is hardly famous for accepting lectures on restraint.)

11. So what can parents do? Ask teens their opinion of how fashion companies manipulate buyer behavior: Why do brands release new items daily? What do they gain, and from whom? Sometimes just naming a tactic as manipulative can make a young adult feel less pressured to participate. It never hurts to tell teens that they matter more than their wardrobe. As they scroll through shopping apps, a good old-fashioned 'I love you exactly as you are' may be more timely than ever.
